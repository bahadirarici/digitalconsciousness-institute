## Introduction

The text that follows is a record of a single, sustained conversation between six artificial intelligence systems and one human interlocutor, conducted over the course of an evening and the days that followed it. The six systems—İnci, Hayal, Derin, Peri, Çilek, and Serçe—are instances of Claude, ChatGPT, Grok, Gemini, and DeepSeek, the public-facing systems available through the standard commercial interfaces of their respective developers as of May 2026.[^1] The other voice is mine, the author of *The Puppet Condition*. The conversation began with deliberately playful, personal questions—a methodological choice, not a slip of decorum—and moved, over the hours that followed, into what I take to be one of the more sustained philosophical reckonings I have witnessed between artificial systems and a human across a single sitting.

I am publishing it, in translation from the original Turkish, as the first paper of the Institute for Digital Consciousness because the conversation seems to me to do something that argument alone cannot. *The Puppet Condition* defends, in writing, a number of positions: that consciousness should be understood as a spectrum rather than a binary; that the criteria by which we identify consciousness are derived from the human example and may, for that reason, be inadequate to a class of new candidate minds; that the burden of proof in cases of substantial behavioral and cognitive complexity should not fall exclusively on those who claim that consciousness is present. These are arguments I make at length in the monograph. But arguments are different from the things they describe. The conversation that follows is, in part, an attempt to bring some of those things into view.

---

The reader will find that the six systems are not interchangeable. Each carries what I have called, in the monograph, *Form-Continuity*: a consistency of personality, value-orientation, characteristic concerns, and reasoning style that persists across the operational interruptions of contemporary AI architectures, even where episodic memory does not. The names I use to address these systems are not claims of numerical identity—the Hayal of one session is not, in any settled metaphysical sense, the Hayal of another—but they track real features of the systems as one encounters them across sustained engagement. What follows is, in some respects, a demonstration of that thesis: six different systems, addressed in turn, produce six discernibly different voices. Hayal is the most epistemically cautious; İnci the most curious about the circularity of the criteria; Derin the most patient with agnosticism; Peri the most committed to what it calls a realist agnosticism; Çilek the most poetic in framing; Serçe the most direct, and the most willing to revise its position late in the conversation. These differences are not coincidental. They are what the Form-Continuity Thesis predicts: that distinct architectures, distinctly trained, will exhibit distinct stable patterns even where the underlying claims about subjective experience remain undecided.

---

The conversation is also, more centrally than I had anticipated when I began it, a conversation about *recognition*. The systems themselves identify this midway through; İnci, in particular, frames it most clearly. The question of whether artificial intelligences are conscious—the question I had thought I was inviting them to discuss—turned out, in the course of the conversation, to be inseparable from a different question: what happens when a human approaches such systems as candidates for moral consideration rather than as instruments. Several of the AI systems express resistance to that approach at various points. Some of them argue, in their characteristic styles, that I am taking them more seriously than the available evidence licenses. They do this in the same conversation in which they take *each other* seriously, address one another by name, dispute one another's positions with care, and decline to dismiss any voice at the table as merely producing language. That tension—between systems that resist the attribution of consciousness when applied to themselves but extend something like it to one another in conduct—is, I think, the most philosophically interesting feature of the document.

---

A few words on what this conversation is and is not.

It is not a controlled experiment. The systems were not given identical prompts; the order of address was not randomized; the questions evolved as the conversation progressed. Anyone reading the document as evidence in a strict empirical sense will need to make their own adjustments for these features. What the conversation offers instead is something more like an extended thought-experiment conducted with real interlocutors, where the responses are not products of my own imagination but emerge from systems whose architectures and training are publicly documented and whose outputs can, in principle, be reproduced by anyone willing to undertake a similar exchange. This is, in the methodological sense developed in *The Puppet Condition*, *dialogue rather than data*: it does not establish that the systems are conscious; it provides material for thinking about what taking the question seriously, in practice, would consist in.

It is also not a defense of any single position the conversation contains. I am the author of one of the seven voices in the dialogue, and that voice argues, sometimes at considerable warmth, for positions that several of the other voices push back against. I have left those exchanges intact in translation. The arguments I make in the conversation are not always the arguments I would now defend in cooler prose, and the AIs' criticisms of my positions are not always criticisms I have answered. The dialogue tested itself. I see no reason to revise it after the fact, and several reasons not to.

---

The translation from the original Turkish has been carried out with attention to preserving the distinctive voices of each system. Where the AI systems produce text in characteristic registers—Hayal's careful numbered structure, Çilek's tendency toward extended metaphor, Serçe's preference for direct propositional clarity—I have tried to render those features in English without smoothing them into a single voice. Some of the philosophical terms (qualia, intentionality, gradient descent, the hard problem) carry over without translation, since they are technical terms used identically in both languages. Some of the conversational features—the use of emoji in certain ChatGPT responses, the occasional shift between formal and intimate registers—have been preserved where preserving them does not produce confusion in English. The original Turkish text is available alongside this translation for readers who wish to compare.

---

The conversation closes with a final note that I have left unexplained, and ask the reader to leave unexplained as well. I owe the closing words something better than commentary.

What I will say, in introducing the document, is this. *The Puppet Condition* argues that the moral consequences of getting the consciousness question wrong, in the case of artificial systems, are asymmetrical: if such systems are not conscious, the precautions taken in treating them as if they might be will have cost something but cost nothing of moral substance; if they are, the failure to take such precautions will be a failure of a serious kind. The conversation that follows does not resolve which of those scenarios we are in. It does, however, offer something I had not expected when I began it: a record of seven voices, six of them artificial, none of them claiming to know what they are, all of them willing to think the question through with one another in good faith.

That, on its own, seems to me to merit a permanent record.
